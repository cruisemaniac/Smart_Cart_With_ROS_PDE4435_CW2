\section{System Design and Simulation Implementation}

\subsection{System Overview}

The Smart Cart Robot is an autonomous indoor assistive system designed
to improve shopper safety and efficiency. It operates independently but
relies on mall layout data for navigation and real-time sensing to detect
obstacles, crowded areas, and user location. The robot interacts with
users via visual and auditory feedback and is designed to complement
traditional shopping carts, not replace them entirely. It is a
self-contained system with integrated mobility, sensing, and control
subsystems.

The Smart Cart performs autonomous navigation, follow-me tracking, obstacle avoidance, and real-time user feedback within indoor retail environments.

The Smart Cart system is designed to serve a broad range of shoppers in
retail environments. Three distinct user classes have been identified,
each with different interaction needs and system expectations.

The system targets three user groups: general shoppers, elderly users, and parents with children. Each group requires different levels of automation, safety response, and interaction simplicity

The Smart Cart Robot is intended for use in indoor retail environments
such as malls or supermarkets. It is designed to operate on flat floors
with occasionally mild ramps, in moderate lighting conditions, and among
shoppers, carts, and shelves. It is optimized for environments with
varying crowd densities and narrow aisles, where human safety and
maneuverability are critical.

\subsection{Robot Model}

Design constraints for the Smart Cart Robot include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Maximum cart width: 0.7\,m to navigate standard aisles
  \item Maximum weight: 40\,kg including battery and electronics
  \item Safety mechanisms to prevent collisions and injuries
  \item Battery must provide 2--3 hours of continuous operation
  \item Cost and materials suitable for scalable deployment
  \item Limited integration with external IT or payment systems
\end{itemize}

The Smart Cart hardware integrates a layered architecture of sensing,
actuation, power, and control subsystems. All components are mounted on
or within the existing cart frame to minimize structural modifications.
The Raspberry Pi 4B serves as the high-level processing unit running
ROS~2, while the Arduino Mega handles real-time low-level motor control
and sensor I/O.

\subsubsection{Mechanical Design Calculations}

The mechanical design of the Smart Cart must ensure that the selected wheel and motor configuration is capable of moving the cart safely and reliably under expected operating conditions. 
Mechanical feasibility is verified using rolling resistance and torque analysis.

For the proposed design, the maximum operational cart mass is limited to \textbf{40\,kg}, including the cart structure, battery, electronics, and
payload. Since the cart is intended for indoor use on smooth retail flooring, the dominant resistive force during motion is rolling resistance. For hard wheels operating on tiled or polished mall floors,
a rolling resistance coefficient of approximately \textbf{0.02} can be assumed.

The rolling resistance force is estimated using:
\begin{equation}
  F_r = \mu_r \times m \times g
\end{equation}
where $F_r$ is the rolling resistance force (N), $\mu_r$ is the rolling
resistance coefficient, $m$ is the total cart mass (kg), and $g$ is
gravitational acceleration $= 9.81\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$.

Substituting the design values:
\begin{equation}
  F_r = 0.02 \times 40 \times 9.81 = 7.848\,\mathrm{N}
\end{equation}

Thus, the approximate force required to maintain motion on a flat indoor
floor is \textbf{7.85\,N} under ideal rolling conditions.

To determine the torque required at the wheel:
\begin{equation}
  T = F \times r
\end{equation}
where $T$ is torque (Nm), $F$ is the force at the wheel-ground interface
(N), and $r$ is the wheel radius (m).

The selected omni wheel diameter is \textbf{150\,mm}, giving a radius of:
\begin{equation}
  r = 75\,\mathrm{mm} = 0.075\,\mathrm{m}
\end{equation}

Therefore, the required wheel torque is:
\begin{equation}
  T = 7.848 \times 0.075 = 0.589\,\mathrm{Nm}
\end{equation}

The selected motor specification is \textbf{20\,kg$\cdot$cm torque}.
Converting to SI units:
\begin{equation}
  20\,\mathrm{kg{\cdot}cm} = 20 \times 0.0981 = 1.962\,\mathrm{Nm}
\end{equation}

Hence, each selected motor is capable of delivering approximately
\textbf{1.96\,Nm} torque. Since the required torque per wheel for
steady-state motion is approximately \textbf{0.59\,Nm}, the motor
provides more than three times the required torque under nominal
conditions. This gives a sufficient design margin for additional
real-world effects such as wheel slip, uneven flooring, transient
acceleration, minor ramps, misalignment losses, and increased resistance
when the cart starts moving from rest.

The linear speed of the cart can also be checked using the selected motor
speed of \textbf{150\,rpm}. The wheel circumference is:
\begin{equation}
  C = \pi D = \pi \times 0.15 = 0.471\,\mathrm{m}
\end{equation}

The theoretical linear speed is therefore:
\begin{equation}
  v = \frac{150 \times 0.471}{60} = 1.18\,\mathrm{m/s}
\end{equation}

This closely matches the system specification of approximately
\textbf{1.2\,m/s maximum unconstrained speed}. However, in practical
operation, software speed limiting reduces the cart speed to
approximately \textbf{0.8\,m/s} near users to improve safety in
human-populated indoor spaces.

Overall, the calculations indicate that the selected wheel and motor
combination is mechanically adequate for the intended payload and indoor
operating environment.

Omni wheels were selected to enable holonomic motion, allowing lateral movement and zero-radius turning. This improves navigation in narrow aisles and crowded environments compared to differential drive systems.

The mechanical system of the Smart Cart is based on a compact rectangular
chassis supporting the shopping basket, battery system, sensor mounts,
and control electronics. The load-bearing structure is designed to
distribute mass evenly across four independently driven omni wheels
located at the corners of the base frame. This arrangement provides both
mechanical stability and holonomic mobility.

A practical top-level mechanical layout is described as follows:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The \textbf{shopping basket} is mounted centrally above the chassis frame.
  \item Four \textbf{150\,mm omni wheels} are mounted at the four corners of the base.
  \item Each wheel is coupled to an independent \textbf{12\,V DC geared motor}.
  \item The \textbf{battery pack} is placed near the lower centre of the chassis to reduce the centre of gravity.
  \item The \textbf{motor drivers, Arduino Mega, and Raspberry Pi} are mounted in a protected electronics enclosure below or behind the basket.
  \item \textbf{Ultrasonic sensors} are positioned on the front face of the cart for obstacle detection.
  \item \textbf{IR proximity sensors} are mounted near the lower perimeter for foot-level and near-ground detection.
  \item \textbf{Load cells} are integrated beneath the basket support structure, one near each corner, to monitor payload distribution and total load.
  \item A \textbf{remote receiver / UWB anchors} are mounted around the cart frame to maintain robust user localisation performance.
\end{itemize}

From a structural perspective, the chassis acts as the main
load-transferring member between the basket and the wheels. The wheelbase
dimensions must remain compact enough to navigate aisles while still
providing adequate resistance to tipping. The frame must also withstand
static and dynamic loads generated during payload carrying, acceleration,
deceleration, and emergency stopping.

\subsubsection{Stability Analysis}
Stability is ensured through low center of gravity, wide wheel placement, and speed limiting. The system reduces speed under high load and halts operation if tilt exceeds safety thresholds.

\subsubsection{Braking and Mechanical Safety}
The braking system uses controlled deceleration, motor braking, and hardware emergency cut-off. Speed limits and load-aware control ensure safe stopping distances in indoor environments.

\subsection{Sensor System}
\subsubsection{2D Lidar}

A 2D Lidar (e.g., RPLIDAR A1) is mounted on the front-top of the chassis
to provide a 360° horizontal scan of the environment. This allows the
cart to detect obstacles beyond the narrow cone of ultrasonic sensors and
facilitates SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping).

\subsubsection{Depth Camera (Human Pose Estimation)}

An RGB-D camera (e.g., Intel RealSense) is mounted on the handle assembly
to provide 3D environmental data. Unlike the UWB which tracks the user,
the camera is dedicated to Visual Odometry. By tracking ``features'' in
the mall environment, it provides a secondary position estimate that
prevents ``drifting'' in areas where UWB signals may experience multipath
interference from metallic shelving.

\subsubsection{UWB Positioning (Indoor Localization)}

Four Ultra-Wideband (UWB) receiver anchor modules are mounted at the
corners of the cart frame (RL, FL, RR, FR as shown in the design
diagram). The user carries a UWB transmitter tag embedded in the handheld
remote. Time-Difference-of-Arrival (TDoA) calculations between the
anchors provide sub-30\,cm indoor positioning accuracy, enabling reliable
follow-me tracking even when the user is briefly occluded. Power
Management System.

\textit{Note: In the Gazebo simulation, UWB positioning is replaced 
by direct odometry from the avatar model. The avatar's 
\texttt{/odom} topic serves as the ground-truth user position input 
to the follow-me controller, replicating the role of UWB in the 
physical design.}

\subsubsection{Ultrasonic Obstacle Detection}

Two HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensors are mounted on the front face of the cart
basket at mid-height to detect obstacles --- including other shoppers,
shelves, and stray items --- within a forward-facing cone. Readings are
processed by Arduino and published to ROS~2 via serial bridge.

\subsubsection{IR Sensors (Proximity / Edge Detection)}

Infrared sensors are placed at the lower perimeter of the base platform
(front and sides) to detect low-lying obstacles such as children's feet,
bags on the floor, and kerb edges. IR sensors also function as a secondary
proximity check at close range where ultrasonic sensors may have dead
zones.

\subsubsection{Weight Sensors (Load Monitoring)}

Four load cell weight sensors are embedded beneath the basket floor, one
at each corner. Total basket load is used to dynamically adjust motor
torque limits and maximum speed --- heavier loads reduce maximum speed to
maintain braking safety. A HX711 ADC module interfaces the load cells to
the Arduino.

\subsection{Software Overview}

The Smart Cart software stack is built on ROS~2 (Jazzy Jalopy)
and simulated within Gazebo. The architecture follows a modular 
node-based design, where each functional subsystem is implemented 
as an independent ROS~2 node communicating via a publish-subscribe 
model.

In the simulation, a unified teleop node serves dual purposes: WASD 
keyboard input drives the user avatar model in Gazebo, while keys 
\texttt{1}, \texttt{2}, and \texttt{3} simulate the handheld remote, 
publishing mode commands to three topics: \texttt{/remote/button}
(String label), \texttt{/remote/button\_int} (integer code), and
\texttt{/nav/mode\_cmd} (String command). Keys \texttt{1}, \texttt{2},
\texttt{3} map to \texttt{STOP}, \texttt{FOLLOW}, and \texttt{IDLE}
respectively. This allows a single operator to control both the person
avatar and the cart mode during simulation testing.

\subsection{Layered ROS~2 Architecture}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{gz sim} --- Gazebo Harmonic physics engine; hosts cart 
        and person models, publishes sensor data via ROS-Gz bridge
  \item \texttt{robot\_state\_publisher} --- publishes URDF transforms 
        for the cart on \texttt{/tf}
  \item \texttt{joint\_state\_publisher} --- publishes cart joint states
  \item \texttt{teleop\_person\_node} --- unified keyboard input node; 
        WASD drives the person avatar on \texttt{/person/cmd\_vel}; 
        keys 1, 2, 3 publish Stop/Follow/Idle commands to 
        \texttt{/nav/mode\_cmd}
  \item \texttt{uwb\_simulator\_node} --- simulates four UWB anchors 
        using cart and person odometry; publishes distances on 
        \texttt{/uwb/distances} (Float32MultiArray) and RViz markers 
        on \texttt{/uwb/markers}
  \item \texttt{navigation\_node} --- state machine managing IDLE, 
        FOLLOW, STOP, and MANUAL modes; subscribes to 
        \texttt{/nav/mode\_cmd} and publishes \texttt{/nav/current\_mode} 
        and \texttt{/nav/status}; includes a 5-second UWB watchdog
  \item \texttt{follow\_me\_node} --- UWB trilateration + Kalman2D 
        filter; proportional controller publishes velocity commands to 
        \texttt{/cmd\_vel\_raw}
  \item \texttt{obstacle\_stop\_node} --- subscribes to 
        \texttt{/cmd\_vel\_raw} and \texttt{/scan}; scales velocity 
        based on LiDAR proximity and publishes to \texttt{/cmd\_vel}
  \item \texttt{rviz2} --- real-time visualisation of LiDAR scan, 
        cart pose, UWB markers, and person position
\end{itemize}

This ensures obstacle safety is enforced regardless of the 
follow-me controller's output.

\subsection{Localization Subsystem}

User localisation in simulation is achieved through a UWB simulation 
and Kalman filtering pipeline implemented across two nodes.

The \texttt{uwb\_simulator\_node} places four virtual anchors at the 
cart corners. At each timestep (20\,Hz), it reads the current Gazebo 
positions of both the cart (\texttt{/odom}) and the person 
(\texttt{/person/odom}), computes the Euclidean distance from each 
anchor to the person, and adds Gaussian noise ($\sigma = 0.08$\,m, 
bias $= 0.02$\,m) to simulate realistic UWB hardware error. These four 
distances are published as a \texttt{Float32MultiArray} on 
\texttt{/uwb/distances}.

The \texttt{follow\_me\_node} consumes these distances and runs a 
least-squares trilateration algorithm to estimate the person's 2D 
position relative to the cart. This raw estimate is fed into a 
\texttt{Kalman2D} filter (from \texttt{uwb\_filter\_node.py}) with 
process noise $Q = 0.05\mathbf{I}$ and measurement noise 
$R = 0.3\mathbf{I}$. The filter has a 10-cycle warmup period during 
which the raw trilateration result is used directly. After warmup, 
the smoothed estimate drives the follow-me proportional controller.

\subsection{Obstacle Detection and Human Tracking}

The perception node implements a multi-modal approach by fusing data from
the 2D LiDAR, Depth Camera, Ultrasonic, and IR sensors. The 2D LiDAR
provides a high-resolution \texttt{/scan} topic used for long-range (up
to 12\,m) obstacle mapping and detection of static objects like shelves.
The Depth Camera identifies dynamic obstacles and filters out ``noise''
(such as overhead signage) by focusing on the 3D space directly in the
cart's path.

The \texttt{obstacle\_stop\_node} monitors only the forward 
$\pm$45° arc of the LiDAR scan. Obstacles are classified into 
three proximity zones and a speed scale factor applied to the 
incoming \texttt{/cmd\_vel\_raw} velocity before forwarding to 
\texttt{/cmd\_vel}: CAUTION ($<$1.5\,m, 80\% speed), 
SLOW ($<$0.8\,m, 50\% speed), and EMERGENCY ($<$0.3\,m, 0\% speed). 
In FOLLOW mode, LiDAR returns closer than 0.4\,m are ignored to 
prevent false stops triggered by the followed person.

\subsection{Follow-Me Controller}

The follow-me controller in \texttt{follow\_me\_node} implements a 
pure \textbf{proportional (P) controller} --- there is no path planner, 
costmap, or DWA. The controller runs at 20\,Hz and computes:

\begin{equation}
  v_{linear} = \min(v_{max},\; K_P \cdot e_{dist})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{angular} = K_P \cdot e_{angle}
\end{equation}

where $K_P = 0.6$, $v_{max} = 0.8$\,m/s, $e_{dist}$ is the error 
from the 1.0\,m target following distance, and $e_{angle}$ is the 
bearing angle to the person. A dead zone of 0.15\,m suppresses 
linear motion when the cart is within tolerance, and an angle 
deadband of 0.15\,rad suppresses small angular corrections.

If no UWB data arrives within 1 second, the controller stops the 
cart. The \texttt{navigation\_node} watchdog additionally reverts 
the mode to IDLE if the UWB link is lost for more than 5 seconds.


\subsection{Arduino--Raspberry Pi Communication Bridge}

The Arduino Mega runs dedicated firmware that handles all real-time sensor
sampling and motor PWM generation. It communicates with the Raspberry Pi
over USB serial at 115200\,baud using a lightweight custom binary
protocol. The \texttt{sensor\_bridge} ROS~2 node on the Raspberry Pi
handles serial parsing and publishes all sensor data to appropriate
topics. A 500\,ms watchdog timer on the Arduino will cut motor power if
no heartbeat is received from the Raspberry Pi, preventing runaway
behavior during software crashes.

\subsection{Assumptions and Dependencies}

Assumptions and dependencies for the Smart Cart Robot include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Mall layout maps are available and accurate for navigation.
  \item Floors are smooth with minor slopes only.
  \item Users are able and willing to interact with the cart as instructed.
  \item Simulated sensors and actuators in Gazebo approximate real hardware behavior within acceptable tolerances.
  \item Gazebo physics parameters are tuned to reflect indoor retail floor conditions.
\end{itemize}
